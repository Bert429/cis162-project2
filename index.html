This site was made for the purpose of informing others about the definition of Lo-Fi and its history, including its presence in society today. It will also include examples of artists and tracks that fit the lo-fi label.

The term Lo-Fi is short for "low fidelity." In the literal sense, it is meant to describe music produced with lower quality recording equipment. However, it has evolved and grown to mean different things as well. In the present day, lo-fi is seen more as a music genre/style that has specific characteristics, the majority being unobtrusive white noise. This can include a grainyness to the audio, imperfections in the instruments being played, thuds and scrapes that would normally be removed before release, and various environmental sounds (birds, people conversing, rain). To some, this brings a sense of authenticity to the music, which was what the original lo-fi movement in the 80's was based on. It is typically lower in energy compared to most music, and most tracks are instrumentals. Some do sample vocals, which also contributes to the low-fidelty aspect of the music. It can bring a certain warmth or feelings of nostalgia to the listener. Lo-fi is associated with other genres such as hip-hop, chillwave, and jazz.

Lo-fi intentionally includes audio imperfections that are avoided in professional settings.
Some artists seek to replicate the feel of listening to music through older technology such as vinyls and cassettes.
Sounds from environments are also heavily used. One popular setting is a typical city street.
Rain is one of the most common environmental sounds used in lo-fi, intending to give a certain warmth to the track.
